0. INTRO


Sri Lanka, which means “holy land” or “resplendent land” in Sinhala, is historically known as Ceylon and also known as the pearl of the Indian Ocean. Boasting gorgeous and abundant natural resources as well as solemn and prosperous cultural heritage, it is the legendary place where Buddha attained enlightenment and nirvana.

Day 1
After traveling across 2.5 time zones and a total flight mileage of approximately 5964.8 kilometers, we arrived at Colombo Airport at 23:35 local time. When I set my foot on this foreign land, a strong cultural difference struck every inch of the skin from the soles of the feet. Surrounded by Southeast Asians’ unique swarthy complexion and confusing languages, the world seemed to be covered with luminosity masks. Overwhelmed with excitement and freshness yet with a little fear, I began my adventure. We took Sam’s bus and arrived at Inspire Village around four in the morning. The first day was spent getting acquainted with the surroundings and adjusting to the jet lag, but we didn’t expect that what bothered us the most was the food problem. Similar to other tropical countries, most of the food here is rice-based, mixed with a lot of spices and coconut-based products. Dining involves mixing rice with curry and other dishes using three fingers gathered as a spoon, with the thumb pushing the food into the mouth. To put it bluntly, yes, they eat with their hands. And oops sorry, I still couldn’t get used to it and dropped some rice grains even on the third day.




Day 2
After breakfast, we went to the bottle house for grouping and started organizing the campsite and leveling the ground. Mixed with of sweat and dust, each of us took dark and heavy hoes and shoveled out every inch of soil under our feet, creating our "homes" after two days of hard work. We happened to reschedule our visit to the national park to today. Having heard rumors of elephants might appear at Sam's place, we became intensely interested in encountering these largest mammals on land. The journey from Sam's place to Minneriya National Park took about two hours. Upon entering the grassland area in a jeep, we were immediately awed by the majestic scenery: the vastness of the lakes, the expanse of the grasslands, and the elephants peacefully grazing. Only when getting close could we feel the tension and relaxation of their muscles and the wrinkles on their skin, carved by the relentless sun over time. That is the imprint left by the primal forces of nature, leaving its mark on a living being with the passage of time.




Day 3
Equipped with three saws and a machete, our mission today was to collect bamboo and remove the "nodes" on it. At first, the task seemed impossible due to the hardness of the nodes (I started to miss the handheld circular saw in the workshop). After a breakdown, I suddenly thought of the idea that we could use yesterday’s hoe to vertically peel along the bamboo fibers, and thankfully it worked! I couldn’t help admiring the local; after learning my method, their strength and precision far surpassed ours. This, I believe, is the gap between rural craftsmen and urban kids.


Three days before the journey, eleven Taiwanese ventured as far as the other side of the Indian Ocean. Perhaps due to the disparity between ideals and reality or the homesickness secretly creeping in, a sense of unease gradually fermented among us.

The population in the region primarily consisted of Sinhalese people and a minority of Tamils. On July 23, 1983, ethnic tensions were manifested in an on-and-off insurgency against the government by the Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE). An LTTE attack on 13 soldiers resulted in the start of a civil war, and it wasn't until 2009 that it was officially declared over. This civil war, lasting for 25 years, had its roots traced back to the British colonial period, sowing the seeds of ethnic discord that sprouted into a prolonged period of conflict, nurturing seeds of strife and animosity.




Day 4
FINALLY, the awaited camp days arrived. Today, I, along with Xin Yuan and Yun Wen, were assigned to the registration team. It was also the first day I met Sathija and I still remember teaching him to say "Thank you for your help and effort. Hang in there!" (辛苦了) in the morning to tease Hsu Kai-ting. In fact, the registration was quite chaotic at the beginning due to the local absence of queuing habits, compounded by language barriers. Not to mention the performance for the opening ceremony, I couldn’t keep up with the moves since I was peeking at my partner.



Day 5
The day that made me fall in love with this place.
It was workshop time in the morning, and in the afternoon, we split into groups to build the house at the campsite. Since the age ranged from 6 to 20, I, along with Sanda, assigned the younger ones to collect materials while the older teenagers and I built the house structure. At first, Sathija, Sethmina, and I went to find materials that can be used as ropes, but by the time we returned, Thola had already completed the main portion of the structure. When we returned for the second time, they had already started looking for banana leaves to decorate, and Sanda had taken the children to open up another campsite.



During this period, you could see many little kids working together to carry blocks, or hear voices calling for others to venture into the forest to find materials. There wasn't a single person idling on the side. Regardless of age, gender, or even race, we were all working hard toward the same goal. My writing may not do justice to my emotions, but I remember telling Yun-Wen more than once, "They are amazing; I'm moved to tears."

GOODBYE


# Home
# Goodbye
# Children

Day 6
Today’s mission was to start a campfire and cook some food. Sanda had already distributed ingredients and utensils that everyone needed. Initially, the boys and I were preparing to build a campfire, while Yun-Wen was preparing ingredients with the girls and the kids. Suddenly, it began to rain heavily, and we rushed to save the ingredients into the house. Some little girls were worried that I would get wet and ran out to assist me. At this moment, Oshan was still trying to tend the fire, and a seven-year-old brother was hiding under the table, holding a bowl and continuously grinding the ingredients. In the evening, Ant shared, "Thanks to the rain that makes our 'home'."



Day 7
I’ve been worried about whether it will rain or not today, and it indeed started pouring around noon. Everyone had to wait patiently inside the kitchen. At that time, a little girl came to tickle me (Tilaxini, my love❤️), and then two, three also came closer, and soon I was surrounded by the girls. I taught them finger games, and they covered my eyes to scare others.




Day 8
It was the last day. In the morning, Sathija seemed to have a long face. I asked him what's wrong, and he said, "I hate this. It's a leaving day." Perhaps reflecting his mood, the weather was still terrible today. Shortly after the morning exercise, it started raining, and due to the damage caused by the monkey the day before, our house began to leak. So, everyone rushed to the nearby cowshed to take shelter from the rain. Many children were braving the rain to save our kitchen utensils and ingredients. They even wanted to move the tires, which was super cute from my viewpoint. In the end, we decided to cook inside the cowshed. In less than 30 minutes, they organized a complete space and slowly lit the fire in the drizzling rain. Faced with unexpected incidents, they didn’t complain or give up. They just accepted it and then moved on. When it was time to leave, I was actually annoyed. Frustrated that there wasn't a perfect ending that I couldn't say a proper goodbye to each of them.

During the camp, I saw various possibilities in the children, as well as their innocence and kindness. I often joked about stuffing them into my suitcase and bringing them back. Now, I regret not doing so.
